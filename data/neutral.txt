The afternoon light settled over the harbor as the last of the fishing boats came in, their hulls low in the water. A worker on the pier coiled a rope with slow, practiced movements, pausing now and then to watch the gulls wheel above the breakwater. Farther along the quay, two children raced their bicycles between stacked crates, and the smell of salt and diesel hung in the cooling air.

Inland, the town kept its own rhythm. Shopkeepers drew down their awnings against the low sun, and a bus sighed to a stop outside the library, letting off a handful of passengers who scattered toward supper. In the square, the fountain ran thin and quiet, its basin littered with a day's worth of copper coins and a single paper boat, half sunk.

The baker on the corner had already begun tomorrow's work. Flour dusted his forearms as he folded the dough over itself, again and again, the bench creaking under the effort. Through the window behind him the street slipped into shadow, and the first lamps came on one by one, each a small amber promise that the evening would be ordinary.

By the time the ferry sounded its horn at the mouth of the bay, most of the town had turned indoors. Radios murmured through open windows. A cat crossed the empty square, unhurried, and took up its post on the warm stone steps of the church. The tide turned. The boats rose gently on their moorings, and the water carried the last light out past the point, where the horizon folded it away without ceremony.

Morning would arrive the way it always did: the clatter of crates on the quay, the hiss of the first espresso machine, the school bell counting the children in. Nothing about the town asked to be remembered, and perhaps that was its particular grace. It went on, tide after tide, indifferent to the ships that passed far offshore and to the news they carried, content to keep its small accounts in order and its windows clean.
